# Muslim Will Homepage

## Page Role

The homepage should do four jobs clearly:
- establish trust within the first screen
- explain the service without legal overclaiming
- show why Muslim families should act now
- move visitors toward `Start Your Will` or `Book a Call`

This page should feel premium, calm, and professionally accountable.

## Brand Direction

### Visual Tone
- premium
- spacious
- modern
- family-first
- faith-conscious
- legally credible

### Brand Colors
- Deep green: `#004926`
- Warm cream: `#eeeedd`
- Gold accent: `#d4af37`

### Design Notes
- use deep green for anchors, headings, and trust cues
- use warm cream for background rhythm and softness
- use gold as a disciplined accent, not as a dominant fill
- keep the interface light and elevated, with soft shadows and clean structure
- avoid generic legal-site clichés, stock-website layouts, and crowded icon grids

## Geographic Positioning

### Launch Position
- homepage message = Canada-first
- strongest legal proof point = Ontario
- future expansion = North-America-ready, including the United States later

### Homepage Rule
The homepage should speak broadly to Muslim families in Canada, while using Ontario as the clearest current legal anchor. It should not read as Ontario-only, and it should not make broad U.S. promises before a real U.S. rollout exists.

## AEO Strategy

The homepage should answer the kinds of full questions people ask ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity.

### Prompt Matches To Cover Naturally On The Page
- How do I create an Islamic will in Canada?
- Is an Islamic will legally valid in Ontario?
- Can I include charity in an Islamic will?
- How do I protect my minor children in a Muslim will?
- What happens if a Muslim dies without a will in Canada?
- Is Muslim Will a law firm?

### AEO Writing Rule
Each major question should be answered with:
1. a direct first sentence
2. a short explanation
3. a link to the deeper page later

## Section 8: AEO FAQ Preview

### H2
`Common questions, answered clearly.`

### Question 1
**Q:** `Is an Islamic will legally valid in Ontario?`

**A:** `An Islamic will can be recognized under Ontario law when it is properly prepared, reviewed, signed, and witnessed according to applicable legal requirements. Muslim Will is designed to support that process with a structured legal workflow.`

### Question 2
**Q:** `How does an Islamic will work in Canada?`

**A:** `An Islamic will in Canada must work within provincial legal rules while reflecting the inheritance intentions of the person creating it. That means the legal structure and the Islamic inheritance structure must be approached together, not separately.`

### Question 3
**Q:** `Can I include charity in my Islamic will?`

**A:** `Yes. Charitable bequests can be included as part of a will, and Muslim Will helps families think through that intention clearly within the permissible bequest framework.`

### Question 4
**Q:** `How do I protect my minor children in a Muslim will?`

**A:** `A will can help parents document guardianship preferences and related family instructions before a crisis. This is one of the most important reasons many Muslim parents begin the process.`

### Question 5
**Q:** `What happens if a Muslim dies without a will in Canada?`

**A:** `If someone dies without a will, provincial intestacy rules may determine how the estate is handled. That can leave families without clear documentation for guardianship, inheritance intentions, or charitable wishes.`

### Question 6
**Q:** `Is Muslim Will a law firm?`

**A:** `No. Muslim Will is a structured technology platform and workflow system. It is not a law firm and does not replace legal advice or religious rulings.`

### Question 7
**Q:** `Does this only work in Ontario?`

**A:** `No. The homepage should speak to Muslim families in Canada more broadly. Ontario is used as the clearest current legal reference point, while deeper regional handling can expand over time.`

### Question 8
**Q:** `Will Muslim Will expand to the United States?`

**A:** `That is a future direction. When U.S. expansion happens, it should be handled through dedicated U.S. and state-aware content rather than broad generic claims, because estate law in the United States is shaped by state-level rules.`
